Good Morning. I am Dr Lucas de Toca and I lead the roll-out of the vaccine through general practice for the National COVID vaccine task force. ACT Health recommends people wear masks inside but acknowledges that for situations in which, for diction or for communication, it is important to see the person's mouth and it is OK to take it off. That is why Linda, who is doing AUSLAN interpretation, and myself will take it off for this segment. Thanks, Linda. Today I will answer your top three questions that have come through social media channels hopefully with answers you're looking for. My shout-out today goes to the Aboriginal community-controlled health services around Australia delivering their vaccines to their communities and the nearly 100,000 Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander people who have received at least one dose of the vaccine already. It is NAIDOC Week this week, so I want to acknowledge we are in the land of the Ngunnawal people. Dhawra nhuna, dhawra Ngunawal.Yanggu gulanyin ngalawiri, dhunayi,Ngunawal dhawra.Wanggarralijinyin mariny balan bugarabang.

Let's go to your questions.

First question, are children more at risk of catching the DeltaCOVID-19 strain?

It has become really apparent that the Delta strain, or B16172, is a far more transmissible strain than the original virus and even more so than some of the other more transmissible stains like the Alpha strain that was first identified in the UK. It is understood that the strains is anything between 40 and 70%, probably about 60% more transmissible than the original virus and that is seen across all age groups including children. What we have seen in other countries overseas where a significant proportion of the adult population has been vaccinated is we have seen a proportionately higher number of cases in younger people and children that in the first, second or third waves those countries suffered. Whether the strain is more transmissible in children or whether it’s a product of the fact that the majority of the adult population has either already had the virus or received a vaccine is still unclear, but what it is clear it is more transmissible in all age groups. Encouragingly, it does not seem that these new strain causes any more severe disease in any age group. While we are seeing more children infected in other countries. We are not seeing worse outcomes from it than what we saw from the original strain of the virus. As with the wildtype, or the original strain, COVID-19 and the risk of severe disease from COVID-19, dramatically increases with age and that is why it is so important that all the people around us are first to be protected with vaccines against COVID-19.

Second question – I'm over 60 years of age. If I speak to my GP, do I have a choice of which vaccine I can receive?

The Australian Technical Advisory Group on Immunisation, ATAGI which used to be a pretty obscure committee last year and now everyone knows about it, has provided advice on the preferential use of Pfizer under 60. The AstraZeneca vaccine is approved for everyone 18 years and over but ATAGI recommended that it is best used in people 60 and over because of the risk benefit equation of the rare but potentially severe side effects from the vaccine versus the risk from severe disease from COVID. There are a limited number of specific circumstances that mean a person 60 years and over cannot receive or is not encouraged to receive an AstraZeneca vaccine. There is a small number of very specific clotting conditions, so not your average thrombosis or clotting event, not any person who has had DVT on a flight or anything like that. We are talking about a small list of very specific, generally immune-mediated thrombotic events or clotting conditions and that list can be found on the Department of Health's website on health.gov.au and of course any person who has had a severe adverse reaction to a first dose of AstraZeneca vaccine, like anaphylaxis or a severe allergic reaction or of course the thrombosis with thrombocytopenia syndrome or TTS, then they are encouraged not get a second dose of the AstraZeneca vaccine. For everyone else of any age who has received the first dose, they are strongly encouraged to receive the second dose of the AstraZeneca vaccine when its due and for everyone else 60 and over, the AstraZeneca vaccine is available to you, noting of course younger people can also get it if they choose to do so after a decision on risk and benefit and full informed consent with a health provider. Ultimately talk to your health practitioner to understand what your specific conditions or circumstances mean in the vaccine rollout. But just to note it’s a small number of conditions that are contraindications or preclude you from getting the AstraZeneca vaccine.

And finally, can my workplace make it mandatory to have the COVID-19 vaccine?

So, this is very  topical because in the last week the National Cabinet agreed it would make it mandatory for people working in residential aged care facilities, so for residential aged care workers to receive the vaccine and that mandate will come into effect in mid-September requiring workers in residential aged care facilities to have at least one dose of the vaccine. That’s done because it is really important we protect our most vulnerable which are by far residents of residential aged care facilities. COVID is more severe in older people and their situation of congregate living makes their risk of transmission higher and protecting the aged care workers themselves, not only provides that direct protection to the worker but an additional layer of protection to the residents in those facilities. That's why National Cabinet took the unprecedented step of making that vaccination mandatory in aged care workers. That follows medical advice from the Australian Health Protection Principle Committee and has a bit of a lag of a couple of months to be implemented so everyone has a chance to receive the vaccine. In other circumstances in some states and territories, other types of frontline workers like people working in quarantine facilities, in the border or in quarantine hotels may also be required to receive the vaccine. The Fair Work Ombudsman’s website has information on all of that if you are interested.

That’s today's questions. Thank you for watching and continuing to engage the channels to submit your questions. We review them every time and respond in each of these segments. Thank you for remaining COVID Safe, enjoy NAIDOC Week and take your time to acknowledge and celebrate the success of your First Nations colleagues, family members and anyone who is watching. Go and get a vaccine if it is offered to you. Thank you.

This table shows the number of COVID-19 cases by source of infection for each state and territory, since the first case was reported. The table also shows the total number of cases and deaths by state and territory.

The source of infection for confirmed cases of COVID-19 can be described as:

  • overseas acquired – the person was infected while overseas (including at sea)
  • locally acquired – known contact – the person was infected in Australia through contact with someone confirmed to have COVID-19
  • locally acquired – unknown contact – the person was infected in Australia, but the source of infection is not known
  • locally acquired –interstate travel – the person was infected in Australia, but not in the reporting jurisdiction
  • under investigation – the source of infection has not yet been determined, but is currently being investigated through public health actions.

The majority of confirmed cases since late October have been overseas acquired.

The number of cases currently under investigation should ideally be as low as possible.

Knowing the source of infection assists in stopping the spread of COVID-19.

A COVID-19 death is defined for surveillance purposes as a death in a probable or confirmed COVID-19 case, unless there is a clear alternative cause of death that cannot be related to COVID19 (e.g. trauma). There should be no period of complete recovery from COVID-19 between illness and death. Where a Coroner’s report is available, these findings are to be observed.

To date, over 13 million tests have been conducted nationally. Of those tests conducted, less than 1% have been positive.
